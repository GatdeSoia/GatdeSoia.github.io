Mars Vice

*That* queer, furry, sci-fi, adventure game.

Developer: Rho Labyrinths. Status: Released | Steam

A biopunk adventure

Mars Vice is a narrative adventure game set on a distant and futuristic Mars. Follow two civil investigators, Dax and Anaya, as they root out deep-seated corruption in the Martian city of Pavonis in this biopunk-infused story.

For this project, I was the 3D art lead, VFX artist and in-game animator. My tasks went from supervising 3D assets production and lighting, to making optimization passes, writing custom shaders, creating particle effects, and rigging and animating organic and inorganic creatures and machines.

Main tools

  • Unity (HLSL, C#)
  • Blender
  • Affinity Designer

Iterating processes, prioritizing production

Throughout development, I refined pipelines and assets reusability.

The game had many unique elements. At times, it was easy to treat each object as a hero asset, and as such, to categorize them all as high priority. Reality was that no matter how much we wanted everything to fit in before release, some things would have to go or exist in a less unique manner.

We had textures reused for multitude of surfaces, and entire assets re-contextualized to use as decor in different areas of the game. The graphic design work (such as the one used for posters and product design) was streamlined by sharing the UV maps with the 2D team. The level designer became very creative with the set-dressing tools I made and the available gallery of 3D assets that our team produced.

I introduced a custom-made triplanar world projection for tilable textures, scrollable ads, holographic texts, many kinds of bodies of water (the cauldron lake, waterfalls and water tanks), and an embarrassingly-good pixel censor.

A mix of 2D and 3D

Everything alive had to be 2D. Everything inorganic was made in 3D.

So plants, characters and actual animals were billboarded towards the camera, whilst the world could be freely navigated within the boundaries of the map (for most scenes). The development of this system fell on the hands of the programmer. One great thing about it is that lighting conditions also affected the sprites. In the end, all elements blended harmoniously on screen.

The art director made sure we kept the visual cohesion of all elements, in all scenes. Having very early in production color concepts and a bunch of character sprites helped us visualize the final art we were aiming for.

But managing a team of artists while also creating assets myself wasn't an easy mix. I ended up blocking entire days for one or the other task, making the mental transition easier for me.

I hope players appreciate the effort. That little robot surely stole my heart while I was animating it.
