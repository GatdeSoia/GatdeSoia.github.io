2. Animation styles 🐬

Who can name an animation style? 💬

Animation style ≈

the aesthetics of motion*

  • * ... and the lack of,
    • like silence is part of the aesthetics of sound and music.
  • The aesthetics of motion in video games are closely related to the ~game feel ✨~

Is pixel art an animation style? 💬

Is pixel art an animation style? 👾

  • I would argue no.
  • Alternative terms:
    • SNES platformer animation:
      • 2 to 3 sprites per animation sequence,
      • "slippery" movement*,
      • max. 4 colors**.
    • 16-bit 2D fighting game animation:
      • many more sprites per animation sequence,
      • rigid, fast-reaction movement*,
      • more colors available (but restricted for readability and optimization)**.
  • Can't separate animation from implementation (*) or art (**).

What about realistic animation? 💬

What about realistic animation?

  • Let's compare:
    • cancelling animations are different*,
    • timing penalty is different*,
    • length of actions is different*.
  • Can't separate animation from design and genre conventions (*).
    • In two of these examples, the conventions are associated to a developer. It's part of their signature.

Anime? Disney? 💬

Anime? Disney? 🏰

  • Know the time period of your inspirations, and the authors and animation houses behind them.
  • To be precise in your work, you need to know the medium of animation and its history.
    • ... this will take some time.

Extra Take

  • Don't call it Ghibli style if it's not antifascist 🐽
    • Repurposing a style you love is great, but knowing its history and participating consciously in the genealogy of art is even better.
  • Form without substance is meaningless.
  • And yes, AI is an insult to life itself.

Medium of animation 📽️

  • ... because it's a means of communication.
  • Animation in games serves to communicate a message to the player:
    • Explicit: a jumping animation communicates the character is jumping. Different idles help convey health status.
    • Implicit: a character's personality. Tone (humor, drama, tension...).
    • Is redundant: more than one part of the animation conveys the message. Subtlety often ends up mudding the result.
    • Is contextual: its meaning depends on the context.

3. Methods & pipelines 🌊

Methods (Techniques)

  • Sprites 👾
  • Skinned 2D 🧸
  • Skinned 3D 🏃🏾‍♀️‍➡️
  • Blend shapes / Shape keys 🔑
  • Non-skinned 3D 🤖
  • Physics-based 💃🏻
  • Via shaders ⛱️

Sprites 👾

  • 1 pose = 1 picture.
    • All pictures get collected in spritesheets.
  • Constrain character movement with draw box limits.
    • Treat the ground/center of mass as the pivot.
  • Two main workflows (always in power of 2*):
    • Traditional animation (each frame is drawn).
    • Any other method rendered/baked in a sequence.

*Power of 2?

  • 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096
  • More, smaller images = worse for the CPU, better for the GPU.
  • Fewer, bigger images = better for the CPU, worse for the GPU.

Skinned 2D 🧸

  • Instead of drawing each frame, characters are designed and sliced into pieces to assemble them like a puppet.
  • Animation done via interpolation:
    • Mark (key) the poses in a timeline.
    • The machine calculates the trajectories ➡️ it interpolates the in-between frames.
  • Facial expressions can be animated via card swaps:
    • A drawing per expression with no interpolation (each pose snap into each other).
    • Can get more complex by having mouth poses separated from eyes and eyebrows.

Skinned 3D 🏃🏾‍♀️‍➡️

  • You have a model:
    • Add a few bones.
    • Weightpaint the model vertices to the bones.
    • Animate by keying the poses (interpolated animation) through transforming the bones (rotating, moving and scaling them).

Best practices: Model 🧊

  • Have a clean mesh.
    • Skinned meshes are x10 times more resource-heavy than static meshes. Each triangle counts.
  • Apply all modifiers.
  • Pivot point on the ground, between the feet.
    • Quadrupeeds: between the front legs or at equal distance between front and back legs.

Best practices: Rig 🏗️

  • Rig = skeleton + controllers.
  • Consistent naming conventions.
    • Create all the bones on one side, name them properly with the side sufix (e.g.: Arm_L, Finger_03_02_R), then symmetrize.
  • Always have a root bone:
    • Both the armature object's pivot and the root bone are at the same location as the mesh's pivot.
    • The root bone will be the character's collision origin.
    • The root bone is the parent of all other bones.

Best practices: Rig Axis 🏗️

  • Consistent axis direction.
  • Quaternions vs. Euler:
    • Quaternions (X, Y, Z, W) are complicated to animate on a timeline and don't work as expected with >180º rotation transforms between keyframes (i.e.: SLERP)...
    • ... but Euler (X, Y, Z) might end up in gimbal lock. To avoid it:
      • Set rotation axis order: #1 the most important rotation axis, #2 the least important rotation axis, #3 the middle rotation axis.
        • E.g.: in Euler.XZY, the bone rotates mainly on its X axis, sometimes on its Y axis, and barely on its Z axis.
  • Lock unused transforms on each bone.

Best practices: Animation 🐎

  • Key poses go first.
  • Don't animate the root bone.
    • For root animation animate the armature object.

Blend shapes / Shape keys 🔑

  • Animate a vertex or a group of vertices through interpolating between two transforms.
  • Useful for:
    • Face animations (no need for bones/reducing the amount of bones).
    • Mesh adjustments in extreme poses (to fix volume distortions).

Non-skinned 3D 🤖

  • Why even?
    • Because it's less resource-heavy.
    • Often used for machinery/mechanical animations.
    • Each piece has its rotation directions aligned and pivot points on the center of rotation.
    • Pieces are stacked by parenting hierarchy.
    • Can still use drivers and constraints.
  • Hand-yielded objects are not part of the character's mesh, but static meshes attached in engine to the armature's bone.

Physics-based 💃🏻

  • The computer calculates the movement based on mass and force:
    • It can stay real-time (only for objects with a lot of interaction).
    • It can be pre-computed and baked into an animation sequence.
  • Use cases: physics as mechanics, hair and clothes, curtains, pre-computed debris.

Via shaders ⛱️

  • Mostly common for water FX.
  • Can animate:
    • Vertices.
    • Textures.
    • Material properties.

Point of view / Distance

  • Sidescroll.
  • Top-down.
  • "Isometric" (axonometric projection, usually dimetric).
  • "Perspective" (linear projection).

  • RTS / MOBA.
  • FPS.
  • Visual novel.

Think of a game... 💭

What's its style? Technique? POV? 💬

Pipelines

  • Game development is iterative...
  • ... and so is animation for games.

Walkthrough 🐾

  • The design department comes to you with a bunch of character actions...
  • Sketch a behavior map of how animation sequences will start, loop or end.
  • Start with key poses.
    • No secondary / overlap movement.
    • For skinned 2D/3D, start off with dummy models (final designs will take a while and you need to start as soon as possible).
  • This way, programming can start implementing and design can give you feedback early on.
    • Refine details after testing timing and readability in context.

  • Set naming conventions.
  • Set deadlines.

4. Setting up priorities 💗

What are your resources?

  • Skill 🦊 (or will to get there).
  • Team 🐜🐜🐜 (or lack of).
  • Time ⌛ (see capital).

What are your responsibilities?

  • It depends.
  • At least:
    • Animate characters.
    • Export the animation assets.
    • Import to engine.
  • Overlapping functions with art, design and programming need to have an owner (someone responsible).
    • You can, and should, review and correct these divisions of work.
  • Everyone in the team should know how to use version control and log their time.

Are you also the artist? 🎨

  • You don't have time.
  • Keep animations at minimum.
  • You will have to leave implementation to the programmer.

Is someone else doing the art? 🎨

  • Take over implementation tasks:
    • Behavior trees.
    • Locomotion blendspace.
  • Programmers are the usual bottleneck (it's not their fault).
    • The more tasks you take from them, the more time they'll have for other parts of the project.

Who makes the rigs? 🏗️

  • You. Most likely it's you.
    • I've been the technical artist and technical animator in some projects, having dedicated artists and animators in the team. This is rare in small indies.
  • Learn to make your own rigs.
  • Art (and animation) is always technical.

Priorities

  • Unique animations are always nice-to-have.
    • First exception: walking/running cycle for player character.

Recycle / Reuse

  • Keep the amount of rigs at minimum.
  • The most important set of animations is:
    • Whichever informs the most about player actions and context feedback.

Cutting corners

  • Cinematics?
    • Illustrations with a bit of camera movement.
  • Dialogue system?
    • Facial / body expressions instead of full lipsync.
  • Multiple characters?
    • Everyone shares the same rig.

Prioritize

  • ... because, no matter what, something won't make it to release.
  • Start rough, then polish.
  • Move! Dance, do martial arts, swim, or go to pilates, but move every day.
    • Yes, this makes you a better animator.
