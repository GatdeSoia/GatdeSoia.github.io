Super RTL Toggo Games

HTML5 game adaptations of Toggo's cartoons.

Developer: Flying Sheep Studios for Super RTL Toggo.

30 games in 3 years

Between 2017 and 2020, I worked as a 3D artist for Flying Sheep Studios, a German game development company focused on HTML5 technology. Its major client was one of the biggest television networks in the country, and we were making many of the games that would go to their kids channel's website. Around half a million children played our games every month.

It was like the 90s again! But instead of Flash and Shockwave, we had HTML5 and Javascript to run everything. 3D for web was not as ubiquitous as today, and our technical restrictions were quite tight, even more so if you account that the games needed to be compatible with older mobile devices (up to 2012!).

In just three years I did from concept art to modelling, texturing and animation, level design and VFX for more than 30 games, most of them as client work for Toggo. Our development cycles were one to three months long, and I would usually be working simultaneously on two or three projects at the same time.

Main tools

  • Three.js

Technical constraints

Have you ever tried animating characters without inverse kinematics?

Was your bone budget capped at 17 per skinned mesh? (And that includes the mandatory root bone.)

Did you wish you could at least have as much polycount target as a PS2 game?

Have you suffered Safari deciding on its own to ignore data from your texture maps?

Welcome to web 3D graphics.

As time passed, glTF standards improved, as so did our internal tools. But the limits we endured to achieve good performance taught every artist to be very intentional with the size and amount of assets we could put in a single scene. I carry this approach with me ever since.

Original designs for existing brands

In a couple of occasions, I was tasked with coming up with new characters. Sometimes it consisted on requested modifications over already-existing characters, like swapping a head full of serpents with a less menacing helmet on my first Ninjago game. I once made a full cast of slimy monsters that an annoying chipmunk would have to defend his house from. And in the summer of 2017, I created the Droplets, cute Rayman-like characters that would become part of Toggo's summer and winter special events.

They may not look like much, but with just 7 bones total and a tiny polycount, you could have 20 kids in real-time online multiplayer throwing water balloons or snowballs at each other without dropping frames.

Check out the Toggo games I worked on as an artist down below. Many of them are still playable nowadays.
